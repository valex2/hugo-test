Advancing Viscerally Unhuman Technology

Sep 1, 2025 4 min read
Progress Technology

A few days ago, a friend asked the following:

I’ve recently been wondering about the politics of unnatural technology. Like, what is the history of normalizing advancements that people find viscerally inhumane? Current technology in this bucket is pro-natal tech, BCI, cultivated meat, etc. Let me know if you have any thoughts or recommendations on what to read.

Two books come to mind that have helped me think about this. The first is Theodore Kaczynski’s Industrial Society and Its Future. Obviously, I disagree with his violent revolutionary approach and much of his ideological framing, but there are two points that I think he nails. First, society picks up technologies and runs with them almost automatically, like a snowflake sparking an avalanche. And second, that as technologies are integrated, the relationship between humans and society evolves in ways that often diminish individual freedom.

Kaczynski describes a human “power process” – having goals, exerting effort to achieve them, and exercising autonomy in how you do so. Fulfilling the power process is a key part of our happiness. In his view, industrial society undercuts this process: our basic needs are met too easily, so we fill the gap with surrogate activities that mimic the feeling of striving but leave us fundamentally unfulfilled. Technologies, once introduced, spread like wildfire. They’re absorbed rapidly because many scientists, engineers, and institutions are incentivized to integrate them into society. He uses the car as an example: automobiles initially expanded mobility, but over time, they also reduced freedom by locking us into licensing regimes, regulatory systems, and built environments that make life almost impossible without them. For him, the paradox is that the more technology we adopt, the less true freedom we retain.

What’s most compelling to me is how he frames this process as self-propagating: systems adjust humans to fit their needs, not the other way around. Think of medicating schoolchildren so they can conform to the classroom system rather than reshaping the system to fit different learning styles. Each step is justified as a rational fix to a social problem, but the cumulative effect is a tightening system of control. He predicts that genetic engineering and other interventions will only deepen this trend, altering humanity itself to better serve the needs of technological society.

The second book is Aldous Huxley’s Brave New World, which describes a post-industrial society where “unnatural” technologies are normalized, forming the foundation of social stability. Unlike Orwell’s 1984, where order is enforced through coercion and surveillance, Huxley imagines a world where contentment is engineered: people are biologically and socially conditioned to embrace their roles, and resistance feels impossible and, more importantly, unpleasant. In many ways, it’s a society optimized for happiness, but one in which happiness has been redefined into something shallow and managed. The book ends with the main character rejecting the entire system in favor of a simpler life.

For me, the heart of the issue is that unnatural technology is seductive. We crave new knowledge and new ways to modulate our existence. The advances you mentioned—pro-natal tech, BCIs, and cultivated meat are incredibly exciting, and they’re also the tools that could enable humanity to organize at the scale needed to solve climate change. But there’s a thin line between being augmented and being supplanted. As much as I love technology, I find the Brave New World scenario deeply unsettling. There has to be a middle ground where technology uplifts us rather than reduces us to managed, dependent beings.

Clarifying that line, making sure our relationship with technology is cooperative rather than one of quiet domination, seems like the key to keeping it human. Decentralized technologies (which can be built/harnessed by small groups of people) are one such axis. Think language, engineered yeast that produces pharmaceuticals when given simple sugars, the radio, blockchain (sans the internet), 3D printers, at-home methane generators, etc.

I think that progress in the types of technology you’re describing is inevitable, and net good; in my ideal world, for centralized technologies, the government should work to ensure that the scope of the advances remains in the public good by regulating how the technology evolves. This necessitates a technician class in government who can see outside their own hubris. Good in theory, hard to implement in practice.
