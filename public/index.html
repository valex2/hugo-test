About me

I’m a Master's student at Stanford University studying electrical engineering with a focus on biotechnology.

I believe that the future of humanity is sweetly nestled in the intersection of these two fields; hardware innovation will enable at-scale distribution of advances in BCIs, health sensing/treatments, and biomanufacturing – technologies that will improve lives at a scale of n » 1,000,000.

I’m particularly interested in developing systems that leverage progress across EE and Biology. I’m also interested in developing coherent technology policy that leaves room for R&D, public buy-in, and accessibility for all.

In my free time, I love spearfishing, playing spikeball, and thinking about things that make me feel small: the brain, the ocean, and the stars.

Neuroscience Research Engineering
Population Activity in Balanced Spiking Neural Networks after Neuron Loss
I designed, developed, and tested an SNN that simulates ischemic attacks within large, biologically-plausible populations of excitatory and inhibitory neurons. By exposing these networks to tasks of various complexities, and sweeping neuroscience-inspired hyperparameters, I discovered that a family of similar coactivity matrices enables latent dynamics capable of leading to the same behavioral output. The size and spread of this family is constrained by the E/I balance of the network, and suggests that robustness to ablation could stem from the existence of multiple solution spaces in a continuous activity manifold.
